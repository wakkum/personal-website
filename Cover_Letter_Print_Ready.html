Dear Editor,

I’m applying for the Open Source Intelligence Reporter position on your International Investigations team. I’ve spent the last six years at Al Jazeera’s Investigative Unit doing exactly what this job description asks for: using satellite imagery, tracking ships and planes, and digging through leaked datasets to anchor high-stakes reporting. I’m ready to bring that toolkit, and the traditional reporting that makes it meaningful, to the Times.

Two recent pieces show the range of my work. Africa’s Ghost Reporters (March 2025) traced a Russian influence campaign that planted hundreds of paid-for articles across Francophone Africa using fictitious journalists. I found the identity of a deceased teacher whose photo was stolen for the lead persona, pulled Cyrillic strings and a Russian phone number from Word metadata, and traced that number back to a Wagner-linked operator. In The Island of Secrets (IRE Award, 2021), I reconstructed India’s covert military build-out on the Mauritian atoll of Agaléga using commercial satellite imagery and shipping data. I’ve used similar methods for All the Prime Minister’s Men, The Cyprus Papers, and The Gold Mafia.

Here are three quick sketches of the kind of stories I’d want to chase from day one. They aren't fully-formed pitches yet, but they show how I approach the beat:

1. The Russian shadow fleet, mapped end-to-end

Iran and Russia keep oil flowing through tankers that go dark and swap flags in the middle of the ocean. I’d start by building a live dataset of AIS gaps, paired with targeted satellite tasking around ship-to-ship transfer zones like the Laconian Gulf or the Strait of Hormuz. The goal is to connect these movements to the Western enablers profiting from the trade—the EU and Gulf ports hosting the bunkering services and P&I cover, and the beneficial owners tucked away in Seychelles shell companies. It’s the Agaléga methodology scaled up, and the Ghost Reporters work taught me how to follow these networks across the messy digital surface they leave behind.

2. Gaza, after the cameras leave

Once active fighting recedes, the satellite record becomes the only honest witness to what was destroyed, what was rebuilt, and what was never rebuilt. I would systematically compare pre-war, wartime and post-war imagery—schools, hospitals, water infrastructure, press buildings—and pair that with chronolocated open-source footage to test the official accounts of specific strikes. Quiet, methodical, accountability-first reporting that does not depend on access.

3. China’s next Agaléga

India is not the only state quietly turning small islands into forward bases. China’s footprint in Djibouti, the Solomons and across the Indian Ocean has been touched on but never fully reconstructed in a way Western readers can see. I would apply the Agaléga method—dual-use civilian projects that are obviously not civilian when you overlay the satellite, shipping and personnel-movement data—to one or two locations where the public record is thinnest.

I bring the technical foundations—OSINT, geolocation, and satellite analysis—with award-winning work to prove them. But beyond the tools, I offer:

  • A routine working method built on maritime and aviation traffic analysis.
  • Experience handling messy, leaked datasets and the high-stakes source protection they require. I was the project lead for Al Jazeera’s SecureDrop implementation, pitching the initiative and working with the Freedom of the Press Foundation to build our network’s secure whistleblower channel.
  • Instincts for visual investigations that ship in text, video, and interactive formats.
  • Custom tooling. I use AI-assisted coding workflows (Claude, Gemini) to build the scrapers and monitors that allow a one-person investigation to run at the scale of a small engineering team—without ever putting sensitive source data into public models.
  • EU citizenship and a full willingness to relocate to whichever bureau makes most sense for the team.

I’d be glad to talk further and show you what I’d chase first.

Sincerely,


Yarno Ritzen
